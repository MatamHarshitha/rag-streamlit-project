ABSTRACT Liver disease is a major cause of death every year. 
Approximately 29 million people suffer from chronic liver conditions and more than 30 million Americans have liver diseases. 
In England, liver disease is the fifth biggest killer after cancer, stroke, and respiratory disease.
 Worldwide, the most common causes of liver disease include chronic hepatitis B and C, alcohol consumption, and non-alcoholic steatohepatitis associated with obesity and metabolic syndrome.
  In India, around one million new liver cirrhosis cases are diagnosed every year. 
  According to the World Health Organization, liver disease is the tenth most common cause of death in India and affects one in five Indians. 
  It causes more deaths than diabetes or road accidents. 
  Liver diseases often do not show obvious symptoms until they are advanced. 
  Several liver function tests are available to evaluate the proper functioning of the liver. 
  KEYWORDS: Liver, Hepatitis, Liver Cirrhosis. INTRODUCTION The liver is the second-largest organ in the human body and performs more than 5,000 functions, including blood clotting, detoxification, nutrient conversion, hormone regulation, fighting infections, regeneration, and metabolizing cholesterol, glucose, and iron. Most people do not pay attention to their liver until something goes wrong. Liver diseases are increasing and now affect one in ten people.
These diseases may be inherited or caused by viruses, chemicals, alcohol, obesity, diabetes, autoimmune reactions, or metabolic disorders. If untreated, liver diseases can become life-threatening, cause permanent damage, affect the bile duct, or lead to liver cancer. Liver Diseases Many liver diseases involve jaundice due to high bilirubin levels.
 Bilirubin is produced when hemoglobin from dead red blood cells breaks down, and is normally removed by the liver through bile. Hepatitis: Inflammation of the liver caused by viruses, toxins, autoimmune diseases, or inherited conditions. Liver Cirrhosis: Fibrous tissue formation that destroys liver cells. Hemochromatosis: Inherited disorder causing iron accumulation. Benign Tumors: Such as adenoma and focal nodular hyperplasia. Liver Cancer: Primary tumors or metastasis from other digestive organs. Wilson’s Disease: Excess copper accumulation.
  Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis: Autoimmune inflammation of bile ducts. 
  Primary Biliary Cirrhosis: Autoimmune destruction of small bile ducts. Budd-Chiari Syndrome: Hepatic vein obstruction. Gilbert’s Syndrome: Genetic disorder affecting bilirubin metabolism. Many pediatric liver diseases also exist. Liver health can be evaluated using enzyme and metabolite tests. Acute Liver Failure Acute liver failure occurs when the liver rapidly loses its ability to function, usually over a few days. It can cause bleeding, brain swelling, and requires emergency hospitalization. Sometimes it is reversible, otherwise liver transplantation is the only cure. Hepatitis Hepatitis refers to swelling and inflammation of the liver, commonly caused by viral infections (A, B, C, D, E). It may also result from autoimmune disorders, alcohol, poisons, toxins, medications like acetaminophen, or inherited disorders such as hemochromatosis or Wilson’s disease. 
  Symptoms include abdominal pain, fatigue, fever, itching, jaundice, nausea, vomiting, and weight loss. Hepatitis A Usually spreads through stool or blood of infected individuals. Symptoms appear 2–6 weeks after infection and include dark urine, fatigue, itching, fever, nausea, pale stools, and jaundice. Hepatitis B Spread through blood and body fluids. Symptoms may appear after six months and include fatigue, muscle pain, nausea, jaundice, dark urine, and liver enlargement. Hepatitis C Spread through infected blood. Symptoms include abdominal pain, ascites, pale stools, dark urine, fatigue, jaundice, and nausea. Hepatitis D Occurs only in individuals already infected with Hepatitis B. Causes worsened symptoms, and spreads through blood, drugs, and sexual activity. Hepatitis E Spread through contaminated drinking water and occasionally contaminated food or blood transfusion. Symptoms include jaundice, nausea, abdominal pain, hepatomegaly, itching, and joint pain. Alagille Syndrome A genetic disorder affecting liver bile drainage, often caused by mutations in the JAG1 gene. 
  Symptoms may appear in infancy or later in life. Alcohol-Related Liver Disease Alcohol damages liver cells. The liver breaks down alcohol, but excessive drinking causes injury. Three stages include fatty liver, alcoholic hepatitis, and alcoholic cirrhosis. Fatty Liver: Fat accumulation with no major symptoms; reversible if alcohol is stopped. Alcoholic Hepatitis: Liver swelling with nausea, pain, fever, and jaundice. Alcoholic Cirrhosis: Permanent scarring; irreversible and may lead to liver failure. Enlarged Liver (Hepatomegaly) Liver enlargement can result from liver disease, infections, fatty liver, alcohol, Wilson’s disease, hemochromatosis, tumors, cysts, or obstruction of bile ducts. Treatment depends on the underlying cause. Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency A hereditary disorder affecting liver and lungs.
   It is the most common genetic cause of liver disease in children. Budd-Chiari Syndrome Caused by clots blocking hepatic veins, often due to clotting disorders such as polycythemia or sickle cell disease. Gilbert’s Syndrome A mild genetic condition where the liver does not properly process bilirubin. Usually harmless and discovered accidentally through blood tests. Liver Hemangioma A benign mass made of blood vessels. Often discovered incidentally and usually does not require treatment. 
  Nonalcoholic Steatohepatitis (NASH) A “silent” liver disease caused by fat deposits, inflammation, and liver damage in people who consume little or no alcohol. Can progress to cirrhosis. Portal Hypertension High blood pressure in the portal vein, commonly due to cirrhosis. Leads to abdominal swelling, confusion, and gastrointestinal bleeding. Hepatic Encephalopathy: Brain dysfunction caused by toxin buildup when the liver fails.
   Ascites: Accumulation of fluid in the abdomen, commonly caused by cirrhosis.
