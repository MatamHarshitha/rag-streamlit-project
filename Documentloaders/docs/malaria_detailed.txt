Malaria – Detailed Overview

Introduction Malaria is a serious and potentially fatal infectious
disease caused by Plasmodium parasites. It is transmitted primarily
through the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. Despite being
preventable and treatable, malaria remains one of the world’s most
significant public health challenges, especially in tropical and
subtropical regions.

Causes and Parasite Types Malaria is caused by five major species of
Plasmodium: 1. Plasmodium falciparum – most deadly and widespread in
Africa. 2. Plasmodium vivax – common in Asia and Latin America, can
cause relapses. 3. Plasmodium malariae – causes chronic infections. 4.
Plasmodium ovale – found mainly in West Africa, can cause relapses. 5.
Plasmodium knowlesi – zoonotic malaria found in Southeast Asia.

Transmission Cycle The malaria transmission cycle involves: - The bite
of an infected Anopheles mosquito. - Release of sporozoites into the
human bloodstream. - Multiplication of parasites in the liver. -
Infection of red blood cells, causing symptoms. - Another mosquito bites
the infected person and carries the parasite forward.

Alternative Transmission Routes: - Blood transfusions - Organ
transplants - Shared needles - Congenital transmission (mother to baby)

Symptoms of Malaria Symptoms typically appear 10–15 days after infection
and may vary depending on the Plasmodium species.

Common symptoms include: - High fever - Chills and intense sweating -
Headache - Muscle and joint pain - Nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea -
Fatigue and weakness

Severe malaria symptoms include: - Severe anemia - Multi-organ failure -
Cerebral malaria (affecting the brain) - Respiratory distress -
Hypoglycemia - Seizures

Risk Groups Certain populations are at higher risk: - Children under
five - Pregnant women - Immunocompromised individuals - People living in
or traveling to endemic regions - Refugee and displaced populations

Prevention Strategies 1. Vector Control - Use of insecticide-treated
mosquito nets (ITNs) - Indoor residual spraying (IRS) - Eliminating
stagnant water where mosquitoes breed

2.  Preventive Medication
    -   Chemoprophylaxis for travelers
    -   Intermittent preventive treatment for pregnant women (IPTp)
    -   Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) for children
3.  Personal Protection
    -   Wearing long-sleeved clothing
    -   Using mosquito repellents
    -   Keeping doors and windows screened

Diagnosis Malaria is diagnosed through: - Microscopic examination of
blood smears - Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) - Polymerase chain reaction
(PCR) testing for accurate species detection

Treatment Treatment depends on the Plasmodium species and severity: -
Artemisinin-based Combination Therapies (ACTs) – first-line treatment -
Chloroquine (where resistance is low) - Primaquine for preventing
relapses of P. vivax and P. ovale - Intravenous artesunate for severe
malaria

Drug resistance, especially to artemisinin, is a growing concern and
requires continuous monitoring.

Complications If not treated promptly, malaria can cause: - Cerebral
malaria - Organ failure (liver, kidneys) - Severe respiratory distress -
Metabolic acidosis - Coma and death

Global Burden - Every year, over 200 million cases are reported
globally. - More than 600,000 malaria-related deaths occur annually. -
Sub-Saharan Africa carries over 94% of global deaths. - Children under
five account for the majority of fatalities.

WHO and Global Efforts Key global strategies include: - WHO Global
Technical Strategy for Malaria (GTS) - Roll Back Malaria Partnership -
Development of malaria vaccines like RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix) and
R21/Matrix-M - Strengthening surveillance and early detection

Conclusion Malaria remains a major global health problem, particularly
in low-income countries. Continued prevention, timely diagnosis,
effective treatment, and global cooperation are essential to reduce its
burden and move toward eventual elimination.
